# Fedor Dmytrovych Berezin

![Fedor Berezin](../assets/images/berezin.jpg)

**Birth:** 1960, Donetsk, Ukrainian SSR
**Occupation:** Former Soviet air-defense missile officer, science fiction writer
**Languages:** Russian
**Notable Works:** *Voyna 2010: Ukrainskiy front*, *Krasnye zvezdy* dilogy, *Voyna 2030* cycle
**Affiliations:** Soviet Air Defense Forces (1981–1991); from 2014, official of the self-proclaimed "Donetsk People's Republic"

## Biography

### A Soldier of a Vanishing Empire

Fedor Berezin was born in Donetsk in 1960 and graduated from the Engels Higher Anti-Aircraft Missile Command School, the Soviet Union's premier training institution for Air Defense Forces officers. From 1981 to 1991 — a decade bracketed by the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan and the dissolution of the USSR — he served as a missile officer in Kazakhstan and the Soviet Far East, in units built to intercept a specifically Western threat: American strategic bombers and cruise missiles.

After his 1991 retirement from active service, Berezin worked as an entrepreneur and, for four years, as a vertical-shaft driller in a Donetsk mine, while establishing himself as a science fiction writer; his early novel *Pepel* ("Ash") placed second in the *Zvyozdny most* competition, the leading post-Soviet venue for Russian-language science fiction.

### "Future War" Fiction

Between the early 2000s and early 2010s, Berezin published a sequence of "future war" novels imagining renewed conflict between Russia and NATO or the United States, including the *Krasnye zvezdy* ("Red Stars") dilogy and its sequel *Yadernyi rassvet* ("Nuclear Dawn"), *Voyna 2010: Ukrainskiy front* ("War 2010: The Ukrainian Front," 2009) and its sequel *Voyna 2011: Protiv NATO* (2010), and the two-volume *Voyna 2030* cycle. These novels, published years before 2014, are notable for their fixation on Donetsk, Luhansk, Mariupol, and Crimea as the specific geography of a coming war, layered with conspiracist plotting about hidden directorates and staged revolutions. Berezin has described the fall of the USSR not as a political transition but as the death of "a whole civilization" he still mourns.

### From Novelist to Combatant

In June 2014, Berezin was named deputy defense minister of the newly proclaimed "Donetsk People's Republic" by Igor Strelkov (Girkin), the militia commander who confirmed the appointment to a journalist with "Confirmed. We'll see how he handles it." Berezin later told *The New Yorker* that moving from novelist to combatant was simple: "In 2009, I had already written a novel about war in the Ukraine." He has remained embedded in the DNR's institutions for over a decade — as deputy defense minister, a tank-battalion officer, and chairman of the DNR's Writers' Union, which he has himself described as "a combat cell." As of 2024 this involvement has produced an in-absentia Ukrainian criminal conviction for collaborationism.

## Note on this entry

Berezin's biography since 2014 is inseparable from his role in the armed conflict in eastern Ukraine, a subject on which academic and journalistic sources remain sharply critical. This entry follows the framing of the dissertation this site draws on, which treats Berezin's pre-2014 military science fiction as a case study in how Cold War-era ideology, denied its intended object by the Soviet collapse, was reprocessed into genre fiction and, ultimately, real political and military action.
