    Writers Who Shaped Ukrainian Sci-Fi

    Sandro Kasyanyuk
    c. 1900 – unknown
    Proto-SF · Techno-Communism

    Factory worker at Kharkiv's Elektrosila plant who became the first known Ukrainian science fiction writer — celebrating machines as living comrades of the proletariat.

    Vasyl Berezhnyi
    1918 – 1988
    Space SF · Utopia

    Journalist, war veteran, and prolific SF novelist whose debut To the Starry Worlds (1956) sold 165,000 copies — a leading figure in Soviet-era Ukrainian space fiction.

    Read biography
    Volodymyr Zayets
    1949 – 2002
    Postmodernism · Paradox

    Pediatrician by day and satirical SF writer by night — master of unexpected plot twists and philosophical paradoxes. Died under mysterious circumstances in Yemen.

    Read biography
    Viktor Polozhyi
    1949 – 2004
    Philosophical SF · Existentialism

    Philologist, literary critic, and SF writer whose collection Solar Wind exemplifies the Golden Age of Ukrainian SF — paradoxical, humorous, and deeply philosophical.

    Context

    A Century of Ukrainian Science Fiction

    Ukrainian science fiction has always been determined by the requirements of the time and the political situation. The fiction of the 1920s promoted the ideology of naive techno-communism — epitomised by Sandro Kasyanyuk, who portrayed machines as companions of the proletariat and technology as the key to a socialist paradise. During the following decades it became obvious that the Soviet project had reached a dead end, and its positivist component had not brought the desired results.

    By the 1940s–1950s, Ukrainian SF transitioned into "short-sighted fiction" — novels focused on technological advancement within Soviet ideology, with the West cast as antagonist. Vasyl Berezhnyi was among its most widely read practitioners: his debut novel sold 165,000 copies and was translated into more than nine languages.

    The 1970s–1980s brought a Golden Age free from heavy-handed propaganda. Writers explored philosophical questions of identity, memory, and artificial intelligence. Volodymyr Zayets and Viktor Polozhyi refined a distinctly Ukrainian voice — paradoxical, ironic, deeply human — that would resonate long after the Soviet collapse.

    Ukrainian SF writers consistently reflected the political and ideological struggles of their time, from industrial utopianism to dystopian critiques of totalitarianism. Their legacy remains a fascinating study of how literature can both serve ideology and challenge it.
